Kitchen Design Styles — Design Guide
General • 2 min read

The design style preference can significantly add to costs. Certain styles like Indian or Rustic use a lot of natural materials while Contemporary or Modern styled kitchens can be built in the most cost-effective way.

Contemporary / Modern (Most Cost-Effective)
Sleek and polished style blending old and new elements. Features geometric lines, minimal decorations, and bold colors. This is the most widely used and cost-effective style. Surfaces are mostly matt, achieved via:
- Laminate (Solid colour or wood grained) — Most cost-effective
- Acrylic (Solid colour or wood grained) — Moderate cost
- Veneer (Wood grained) — High cost
- PU (Solid colour) — Expensive

Cottage / Country / Beach
Packed with quirky, rustic details that restore original kitchen character. Uses nautical or industrial lighting, traditional cabinetry with decorative details. In India, typically adapted to include country-side cabinetry looks. Shape and contour craftsmanship adds to cost.

Industrial
Rugged modern style with large windows, high ceilings, exposed brick, beams, and piping. Uses textured materials like metal and wood. Generally adopted in studio apartments.

Mediterranean
Inspired by Greek design. Uses earth tones, bold colors, intricate tile mosaics, terra cotta accents, and hardwood furniture. Contemporary geometric lines make cabinetry affordable, but solid wood accents add cost.

Transitional
Blends traditional and contemporary elements. Features Shaker-style cabinets, subtle detailing, a mix of wooden elements. Popular in white and pale neutrals, often with dual colour schemes on cabinets and islands.

Traditional
Features detailed embellishments, raised door panels, decorative crown molding, wainscoting, and solid wood shutters. Requires significant craftsmanship — most expensive of all styles.

Kitchen Layout Guide — General • 3 min read

A well-designed kitchen layout ensures optimal workflow and efficiency. Whether purchasing a ready-made apartment or building independently, understanding your layout helps maximise the efficiency of existing or planned spaces.

A Kitchen is Typically Divided into 3 Areas:
1. Storage Area: Consumables (food grains, pulses) + Non-consumables (utensils, appliances)
2. Preparation Area: Cutting, grinding, mixing
3. Cooking Area: Centred around the cooktop

The "Work Triangle" — positioning the stove, sink, and refrigerator to create a functional triangle — reduces unnecessary movement and creates a streamlined cooking experience.

Common Layout Types:

U-Shape Layout
Three walls of cabinetry and work surface. Maximises storage, provides generous counter space, great for large kitchens. The cook is surrounded on three sides — ideal for Indian cooking which requires multiple active zones simultaneously.

L-Shape Layout
Two adjacent walls used for cabinetry. Versatile and open, leaves space for a dining table or island in the middle. A practical choice for medium-sized Indian kitchens.

Galley Layout
Two parallel walls facing each other. Extremely efficient for smaller spaces. Everything is within arm's reach, reducing movement during cooking.

Island Layout
Any of the above with a freestanding island counter in the centre. Adds prep space, dining area, or social hub. Can include sinks, cooktops, or seating areas.

Parallel Layout
Similar to galley but slightly wider, often used in open-plan Indian homes.

Kitchen Shutter Finishes — Kitchen Cabinets Guide

Choosing the right material for kitchen shutters is crucial as it affects overall aesthetics, durability, maintenance, and budget. Five popular options:

1. Laminate (Most Cost-Effective)
- Surface: Decorative paper bonded to a core board
- Finish: Matt or textured
- Maintenance: Very low — wipe clean with a damp cloth
- Cost: Lowest among all shutter types
- Best for: Budget-conscious homeowners, secondary storage areas
- Available in wide range of solid colours and wood grain patterns

2. Acrylic
- Surface: High-gloss acrylic sheet bonded to HDHMR or MDF
- Finish: High-shine, mirror-like gloss
- Maintenance: Moderate — shows fingerprints, needs regular wiping
- Cost: Moderate
- Best for: Modern, glamorous kitchens where a glossy premium look is desired

3. Veneer
- Surface: Real wood slice bonded to a core
- Finish: Natural wood grain, warm aesthetic
- Maintenance: Higher — needs polishing, avoid moisture
- Cost: High
- Best for: Premium, natural-look kitchens

4. PU (Polyurethane) Finish
- Surface: Sprayed polyurethane coating on MDF or HDHMR
- Finish: Solid colour, very smooth and uniform
- Maintenance: Moderate — avoid harsh chemicals
- Cost: Expensive
- Best for: Those seeking a flawless, painted look with consistent colour

5. Membrane / Thermofoil
- Vinyl wrap applied to MDF
- Good for routed (profiled) doors in traditional styles
- Moderate cost and durability

Countertop Guide — General • 6 min read

Countertops not only serve functional purposes but also contribute to the overall aesthetic appeal of your space.

VITRIFIED COUNTERTOPS — Affordable Durability
Material: Clay, sand, and resins fired at high temperatures
Thickness: Available in 12mm and 15mm slabs
Durability: Hard surface but brittle — may crack under heavy impact; rework is difficult
Stain Resistance: Excellent
Scratch Resistance: Excellent
Heat Resistance: Good
Price: Low — most budget-friendly option
Availability: Widely available
Colours & Patterns: Wide variety
Nosing Options: Sandwich & 45-degree
Best For: Budget-conscious homeowners; surfaces with minimal heavy object impact

QUARTZ COUNTERTOPS — Durability Meets Elegance
Material: ~90% natural quartz crystals + resins, pigments
Thickness: Available in 15mm and 20mm slabs
Durability: Very hard and durable; non-porous surface
Stain Resistance: Good — non-porous makes it highly stain-resistant
Scratch Resistance: Good
Heat Resistance: Moderate — avoid direct extreme heat; do not place hot vessels directly
Price: Moderate to very expensive (varies by brand; Caesar Stone at premium end)
Availability: Widely available
Colours & Patterns: Extensive range imitating natural stone, solid hues, and concrete
Nosing Options: Bull nose, 45-degree, Sandwich, and other types
Maintenance: Regular cleaning with mild soap and water
NOTE: Quartz/Marble are porous — citrus juices (lime) or turmeric can cause permanent stains. Wipe spills immediately.

GRANITE COUNTERTOPS — Timeless Natural Beauty
Material: Natural stone — each slab is unique
Thickness: Available in 18mm and 25mm slabs
Durability: Very hard and durable; heat and scratch-resistant
Stain Resistance: Very good (requires periodic sealing)
Scratch Resistance: Good
Heat Resistance: Excellent
Price: Low to Moderate
Availability: Black granite widely available; other colours region-specific
Colours & Patterns: Limited variety; natural patterns
Nosing Options: Bull nose, 45-degree, Sandwich, and other types
Note: Heavier than marble or quartz — may need extra structural support

OTHER COUNTERTOP OPTIONS:

Laminate Countertops
Available in 3mm, 6mm, and 12mm thickness. Wide range of colours, patterns, and textures mimicking stone, wood, or metal. Easy to clean, resistant to stains and moisture. Most affordable option but not as durable as stone.

Butcher Block / Wooden Countertops
Available in 20mm, 30mm, and 40mm thickness. Natural appearance, warm and elegant. Requires regular maintenance, oiling, and protection from moisture. Not suitable for heavily wet areas without treatment. Rich tones — mahogany, maple, oak.

Concrete Countertops
Highly customisable with colours, textures, and finishes. Durable and stylish, suits modern and industrial kitchen styles. Can be prone to staining without proper sealing.

Porcelain Countertops
Created by wet grinding of clayish raw materials and ceramic pigments, fired at 1200°C. Dense and non-porous — highly resistant to water and bacteria. Available in 3mm and 12mm thickness (cladding: 3m × 1m; kitchen: 3.2m × 1.6m). Light in weight, high durability. Expensive.

Soapstone Countertops
Metamorphic rock composed of 60–70% magnesium-rich talc. Resists stains and bacteria effectively. Predominantly grey with veins in black, blue, brown, white, green. Develops a natural patina over time. Available in 20mm thickness. Cost: ₹4,000–₹6,000 per square foot.

Stainless Steel Countertops
Sleek, modern, and hygienic. Resistant to heat, stains, and bacteria. Non-porous and easy to clean with soap and water. Common in professional kitchens. SS sheets are pasted on a solid surface. Expensive to fabricate — rates vary from ₹1,000–₹1,900 per sq ft.

Quick Comparison Summary:
- Best for budget: Vitrified or Laminate
- Best for elegance & low maintenance: Quartz
- Best for timeless natural look: Granite
- Best for heat resistance: Granite or Stainless Steel
- Best for unique character: Soapstone or Wood

Core Materials — Infographics • 5 min read

One of the most ESSENTIAL core products in the wood manufacturing industry in India are PLYWOOD & HDHMR boards. Particle Boards are generally used in large-scale bulk manufacturing industries.

PLYWOOD
Plywood is a widely used engineered wood product, created by gluing together thin layers of wood veneers. Each ply is bonded with its grain running at right angles to the adjacent layers, providing exceptional strength and stability.

Components of Plywood:
- Core Veneer: Wood used in ply core — generally Eucalyptus, Mango, Neem. Ensures density and strength.
- Surface Veneer: Ensures bond strength between pasting of surface material like laminate, acrylic, veneer.
- Adhesive: Ensures bond between ply surface and laminate/acrylic. Types:
  - Urea-formaldehyde (UF) — Inexpensive but Toxic
  - Phenol-formaldehyde (PF) — Moderate price but harmful
  - Melamine-urea-formaldehyde (MUF) — Expensive but least harmful ✅ (HK uses MUF)

IS Standards Followed by HouseKraft's manufacturers:
- IS 303: Moisture Resistant Plywood
- IS 710: Boiling Waterproof Plywood
- IS 848: Standard for Adhesive

HDHMR BOARD (High-Density High Moisture Resistant)
Created by compressing hardwood fibres with a special adhesive under high pressure. Results in a dense, durable, and moisture-resistant board. Perfect for applications requiring robustness and resistance to moisture — kitchens, bathrooms, and other high-moisture environments.

PARTICLE BOARD
Created by combining wood chips, sawmill shavings, or sawdust with a synthetic resin and subjecting them to compression. Lightweight and affordable, but lacks the strength and durability of plywood or HDHMR. Lower density makes it susceptible to damage from moisture and heavy objects. Used primarily in large-scale bulk manufacturing.

STRENGTH COMPARISON:
- Structural Integrity: Plywood > HDHMR > Particle Board
- Load-Bearing: Plywood is best for structural applications. HDHMR handles moisture well. Particle board is not suitable for weight-bearing.
- Resistance to Stress: Plywood resists warping/cracking best due to cross-grain construction. HDHMR is highly moisture-resistant. Particle board is most prone to damage.
- Summary: Plywood is generally stronger and more durable, making it the preferred choice for cabinetry requiring structural strength.

COST:
- HDHMR: Generally more expensive than plywood (advanced manufacturing, enhanced properties)
- Plywood: More budget-friendly with adequate performance
- Particle Board: Cheapest, but lowest performance

APPLICATIONS:

Cabinetry:
- HDHMR: Perfect for kitchen and bathroom shutters where surface evenness and moisture resistance are crucial
- Plywood: Commonly used for cabinet carcass; may need BWP Grade treatment in moisture-prone areas

Wall & Ceiling Panels:
- HDHMR: High-quality finish for decorative panels finished using pigmentation processes (Paint/Deco/PU)
- Plywood: Versatile for wall panelling and false ceilings where laminate or veneer is used

INSTALLATION & WORKABILITY:
- HDHMR: Heavier and denser — requires specialised tools for cutting and shaping
- Plywood: Easier to cut and shape with standard woodworking tools

HOUSEKRAFT-SPECIFIC FAQ:

Q1: What type/grade of ply does HK use?
HK uses Mixed Core Ply, manufactured to their specifications with manufacturers across the state.

Q2: What thickness of ply does HK use?
Primarily 16mm and 18mm ply with core veneer and surface veneer being Gurjan/Okuma face — which is calibrated.

Q3: What is Calibrated Plywood?
Calibrated plywood maintains consistent thickness throughout, achieved through modern high-end machine calibration. Its uniform thickness makes it ideal for mechanised, automated furniture manufacturing.

Q4: Why does HK use customised plywood?
For quality control and to balance quality vs affordability. Also, warranty claiming for 3rd-party products is cumbersome since the ply is laminated on both sides.

Q5: Can plywood be harmful?
Yes — plywood is produced with resin bonds (chemicals). HK's manufacturers use MUF adhesives which are the least harmful. Avoid UF adhesives which are toxic.

Q6: Why doesn't HK use third-party ply?
Because third-party plywood specifications vary widely, and the warranty claiming process is cumbersome. A manufacturer may use the least expensive adhesive to keep costs low, or may not calibrate the plywood, affecting panel finish quality.

Q7: Does plywood bend?
Yes — for two main reasons:
1. Moisture difference between the two faces: If one face absorbs moisture and the other doesn't, asymmetric expansion causes bending.
2. Laminate on only one side: Plywood is manufactured with an odd number of layers. Sticking laminate to only one side disrupts the structural balance, causing the ply to bow.

Q8: How can bends be corrected?
Bends on shutters can be corrected using shutter straighteners.

Harmonising Hues — General • 2 min read

Colours are not just decorative elements; they are potent tools that can dramatically transform your living spaces.

Colour Psychology:
- Blue: Tranquillity and calmness
- Red: Passion and intensity
- Green: Nature and rejuvenation
- Yellow: Happiness and creativity

Colour Schemes:
1. Monochromatic: Different shades, tints, or tones of a single colour. Creates a clean, elegant, cohesive look.
2. Analogous: Colours adjacent on the colour wheel. More variety while maintaining harmony.
3. Complementary: Opposite colours on the colour wheel. Vibrant and exciting, makes each colour stand out.
4. Triadic: Three colours evenly spaced on the colour wheel. Balanced and more vibrant than analogous.
5. Split-Complementary: A base colour plus the two colours adjacent to its complement. High contrast without the intensity of full complementary.

The 60-30-10 Rule:
60% — Dominant colour (walls/large surfaces)
30% — Secondary colour (furniture/textiles)
10% — Accent colour (cushions/decor/accessories)
This maintains proportion and creates a layered, visually appealing effect.

Key Principle: Let each room's function guide colour choice — invigorating for living areas, calming for bedrooms. Start small (a single wall or furniture piece) and observe the impact before committing to an entire space.

HARDWARE CARE & PRECAUTIONS:

- DIY solution: 50:50 mix of white vinegar and water
- Clean regularly to avoid oil and grime build-up, especially near cooking areas
